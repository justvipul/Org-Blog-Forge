* A Trek That Taught Me About Fear and Strength
Today’s trek was special — our group of 13 hikers included Kailas Sir, a 64‑year‑old veteran who had conquered many forts like Durg Bhandar, Kalsubai, and others. Though it had been a year since his last trek, his enthusiasm and confidence were contagious.

The trail started quietly through a dense jungle, followed by grassy terrain and loose soil paths, and finally led into a valley filled with unstable rocks. The challenge grew with every step. Unfortunately, Kailas Sir’s shoes had little grip, making the climb tough. He relied heavily on his arm strength to pull himself up steep rock patches, which quickly drained him.

Midway through the ascent, his exhaustion peaked — he could barely move. We decided it was best for him to rest safely before the rock section while we continued to the top. On our way back, we found him recovering and relieved to see us. Though everything ended safely, he later shared how he had truly felt the thin line between life and death out there.

That moment reminded me how trekking reveals a unique kind of fear — the fear of falling, of failing, of nature’s raw power. At first, it terrifies you. But as you face it repeatedly, one step after another, it becomes familiar. You don’t stop fearing, but you learn to move through fear.

Trekking humbles you. After facing cliffs and slippery paths where a single wrong step could end everything, everyday worries — about work, money, or social expectations — start to feel lighter. The mountains teach you that fear isn’t an enemy; it’s a guide toward strength and awareness.
